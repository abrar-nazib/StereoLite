\begin{abstract}
Deep stereo matching has, in less than a decade, evolved from learned matching costs (MC-CNN, 2016) through end-to-end 3D cost-volume regression (PSMNet, GA-Net) and iterative refinement (RAFT-Stereo, IGEV), and most recently into the foundation-model era (DEFOM-Stereo, FoundationStereo, MonSter), where monocular depth foundations such as Depth Anything V2 are fused with stereo matchers to deliver substantial zero-shot cross-domain accuracy. These foundation-quality models, however, exceed practical edge-deployment budgets by an order of magnitude or more.

This survey focuses specifically on the body of work concerned with bringing foundation-quality stereo accuracy to real-time edge deployment. We review the field through the lens of \emph{compression}: we identify seven distinct families of compression techniques --- backbone substitution, cost-volume compression, iterative-loop compression, knowledge distillation, architectural compression, adaptive compute, and neural architecture search --- and survey representative methods within each family. We further examine cross-domain generalization techniques that are particularly relevant when fine-tuning a compressed model on limited target-domain data, and we summarize the limited but growing body of edge-hardware validation. Finally, we identify a clear open gap: there is, to date, no published deep stereo model that achieves foundation-model zero-shot accuracy at the latency budgets of contemporary embedded accelerators such as the NVIDIA Jetson Orin Nano. We close with a roadmap describing the architectural and training-recipe combinations most likely to fill this gap.
\end{abstract}
